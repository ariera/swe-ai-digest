GLM-5.1: Towards Long-Horizon Tasks (2026-04-07)

Simon Willison evaluates Z.ai's GLM-5.1, a 754B-parameter MIT-licensed open-weights model, using his standard "pelican on a bicycle" SVG generation benchmark. Willison considers this his favourite output from an open-weights model to date. The result matters to engineers evaluating open-weight alternatives to proprietary frontier models: GLM-5.1 demonstrates strong generative and self-correcting code capabilities at a scale that is freely available via OpenRouter.

Willison covers Anthropic's decision to withhold Claude Mythos from general release under Project Glasswing, restricting access to vetted security research partners. Eight years of wanting, three months of building with AI (2026-04-05)

Willison highlights Lalit Maganti's detailed account of building syntaqlite — a full SQLite parser, formatter, and verifier — using Claude Code. The key finding is nuanced: AI was excellent at handling the 400+ grammar rules needed to bootstrap a working prototype quickly, overcoming years of procrastination. However, AI actively hindered architectural decision-making: cheap refactoring enabled deferred design choices that "corroded" the author's ability to think clearly, and the initial AI-generated architecture had to be discarded and rebuilt from scratch with heavy human oversight. The central insight — that AI excels at implementation with verifiable outputs but struggles with design problems that lack objectively checkable answers — has direct implications for how engineers should structure agentic workflows.
